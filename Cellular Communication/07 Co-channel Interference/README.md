# Co-channel Interference

Even if same-frequency cells are far apart, some unwanted signal mixing (interference) still happens due to non-zero signal strength of such cells which is called Co-channel Interference. However, we can reduce this in mainly two ways. Such as:

i. Cell Splitting  
ii. Cell Sectoring  

---

## 📌 Cell Splitting

One big cell with its own Base Station, divide into many smaller cells is called Cell Splitting.

### Key Points about Cell Splitting:

• Each small cell has its own Base Station (BS)  
• Smaller area → less transmit power needed  
• Less power → less interference  
• More cells → more users can be served (higher capacity)  
• Antenna height and power are reduced  
• Easy to expand network step by step (modular growth)  

👉 In short, Cell Splitting means Breaking big cells into small ones for better capacity + less interference  

---

## 📡 Cell Sectoring

Cell sectoring means, instead of covering full 360° area, divide the cell into sectors or parts. (Sectorization)

### Types:

• Omni = 360° (full coverage) via Omnidirectional Antennas (which allow transmission of radio signals with equal power in all directions). It is difficult to design such antenna. That’s why we use Directional Antennas with (60 or 120 degree coverages)  
• 120° → 3 sectors  
• 90° → 4 sectors  
• 60° → 6 sectors  

👉 It uses directional antennas instead of one big antenna  

---

## ✅ Benefits of Sectoring:

• Each sector covers smaller area → less power needed  
• Reduces interference  
• Improves C/I (Carrier-to-Interference ratio)  
• Better spectrum efficiency  

👉 In real life, Multiple sector antennas are placed on one tower to cover full 360°.  

---

## 🧠 Final words:

• Cell Splitting → increase capacity by making more cells  
• Sectoring → reduce interference by controlling signal direction
